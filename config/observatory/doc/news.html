8 March 2018 | by George Busby blah. blah. blah. 8 March 2018 | by George Busby Humans have been getting malaria for thousands of years. The only way that you can become infected with malaria is to be bitten by an infected mosquito. After this transmission from mosquito to human host, the parasite enters the bloodstream, travelling to and infecting the liver. At this point, a massive population expansion happens and the number of parasites within a person explodes, causing the body to raise its temperature in effort to boil the invader. Growth in parasite numbers is the result of many rounds of multiplication. Each round involves the duplication of each parasite's entire genome. As each parasite does this, one or two mistakes, known as mutations, can creep in. So the generation of so many new parasites creates novel genetic diversity. And, although the mutations underlying this diversity are normally benign or even detrimental to the parasite, very occasionally a mutation can give a parasite an advantage. New genetic diversity makes the evolution of drug resistance possible It is this churn in new genetic diversity that goes some way to explaining how drug resistance evolves. Millions of random mutations are generated over the course of an infection, and given that hundreds of millions of people are infected with malaria every year, sooner or later one that gives a parasite resistance to the drug being used will occur. Unlike other parasites in an infection, a parasite with a resistance mutation will be left unaffected by a drug, and rather than dying will continue to circulate around the human host. If a parasite that has evolved resistance, or more likely its offspring, are present in the bloodstream when the infected host next gets bitten, then the drug resistant parasite has the potential to move between people and resistance can spread. Linking a parasite's DNA to drug resistance with genetic markers The best way to test whether a parasite is resistant to a drug is to challenge them with the drug and see how it responds. One common way of doing this is to administer a drug and then count the number of parasites in the blood stream measured every hour afterwards to see how long the drug takes to clear the parasite from the blood stream. Whilst this allows us to identify resistance in the first place, because it is time intensive and requires lab resources, it is impractical to do on a large-scale basis. Because resistance is the result of changes to the genetic code, an effective way to look at resistance across many individuals it to look at their DNA. This is the approach that we have taken with MalariaGEN Analytics. We can look at a parasite's DNA to see whether it has known resistance mutations and predict which antimalarial drugs it is resistance too. Analysis of DNA from many different parasites can also teach us about other characteristics, such as their geographical origins.
